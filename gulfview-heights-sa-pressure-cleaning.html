Specialists In The Pressure Cleaning Industry

  • Pressure vs. Power Washing
    While both methods use high-pressure water to clean surfaces, the primary difference is that power washing involves heating the water. This makes power washing particularly effective for removing substances like grease or moss, whereas pressure washing uses cold water for general surface cleaning.
    Source: Wash Docs, Squeegee Squad

  • Soft Washing
    Soft washing is a technique that uses low-pressure water combined with biodegradable cleaning solutions to clean more delicate surfaces, such as roof shingles and siding. This method is preferred for surfaces that could be damaged by high pressure, and it’s ideal for preventing the regrowth of mold and algae.
    Source: Imperial Cleaning, Cleaner Times

  • Environmental Considerations
    Pressure washing can often be conducted without harsh chemicals, making it a more environmentally friendly option. However, it consumes large amounts of water, and improper management of runoff can contribute to pollution. Soft washing, with its gentler application, is typically considered more eco-friendly.
    Source: Wash Docs

  • Key Components of a Pressure Washer
    A typical pressure washer includes a water pump, motor or engine, high-pressure hose, and a nozzle or spray wand. These components work together to create the force necessary to clean surfaces effectively. Adjusting the pressure and nozzle type allows for different cleaning tasks, from removing grease to cleaning delicate surfaces.
    Source: Squeegee Squad

  • Water Temperature and Cleaning
    Hot water is often used in pressure washing to enhance the cleaning process, especially when dealing with grease or oil. However, not all surfaces can withstand high temperatures, so it's important to choose the right method based on the material being cleaned.
    Source: Squeegee Squad
